# A2: Thai Real Estate Market Landscape — Northern Thailand / Chiang Mai Lens

**Scope note.** This artifact gates five concrete product decisions: (1) which property segments and price tiers to support at launch (schema fields, filter facets); (2) which buyer personas to design search UX and copy for; (3) whether an Automated Valuation Model (AVM) is feasible with available Thai data sources and what inputs it would use; (4) content and seeding strategy for the public listing catalog; and (5) which languages/formats to prioritize in listing cards and broker-facing flows. Research covers 2024–2026 with a Chiang Mai focus; national data is included where provincial data is unavailable.

---

## 1. Thought Leaders & Sources Consulted

1. **Real Estate Information Center (REIC) — reic.or.th.** State-owned under the Housing Finance Office; the authoritative source for Thai residential transfer statistics, unsold inventory, and price indices. Publishes quarterly and annual reports drawing on Land Department transfer records and National Statistical Office data. Cited by every credible market report in this sector. Used directly via REIC website and Nation Thailand REIC-attributed coverage.

2. **Chiang Mai Real Estate Association (สมาคมอสังหาริมทรัพย์เชียงใหม่).** The provincial trade body whose president, Akkadet Udomsirithamrong, is the named primary source for Chiang Mai-specific new-launch figures (H1 2025), absorption rates, and Chinese buyer concerns. Cited in Nation Thailand (Sep 2025) and BenaNews/RFA (Nov 2024). The only institution producing granular CNX-specific supply-side data not aggregated into national figures.

3. **Bank of Thailand (BOT) — Residential Property Price Index (RPPI).** Monthly hedonic regression index covering detached houses and townhouses nationwide + condominiums for Bangkok/vicinity (80%+ of condo mortgage volume). Published at app.bot.or.th; methodology paper available (Dec 2023). Authoritative for price trend direction; limited for provincial condo data.

4. **BenaNews / Radio Free Asia (Nov 2024) + Nation Thailand (2024–2025).** Investigative journalism covering Chinese buyer dynamics in Chiang Mai with named sources (Chiang Mai Real Estate Association, Chiang Mai University academics, The Glocal survey). More current and geographically specific than any consultant report reviewed.

5. **Bamboo Routes (bambooroutes.com) — Chiang Mai RE blog (2025–2026).** Practitioner-level synthesis aggregating DDProperty, FazWaz, Hipflat, Bank of Thailand RPPI, CBRE, and agent interviews into neighborhood-level price tables. Caveated as secondary synthesis, but the most complete CNX-specific price-per-sqm table found; cross-checked against portal data.

## 2. Findings

### Supply & New Launches

1. In H1 2025, only **284 new residential units were launched** in Chiang Mai — a **66.2% drop in unit count** and **74% decline in value** year-on-year. Subdivisions fell 76.8%; condominiums 52.6%; only villa sales rose (~700%). (Chiang Mai Real Estate Association, via Nation Thailand, Sep 2025)

2. The **absorption rate in August 2025 was 1.6% per month**, implying **~57 months (nearly 5 years) to clear** existing inventory, up from ~47 months the prior year. Unsold inventory rose 4.3% in that period. (Chiang Mai Real Estate Association / Nation Thailand, Sep 2025)

3. Nationally, **REIC recorded 65,276 residential transfers in Q1 2025, down 10.52% year-on-year** in units and 13.02% in value. Full-year 2025 is projected at 322,500 units (down 7.3% vs 2024); KKP Research puts the steepest risk at a total market value potentially falling to 700 billion baht — the sharpest contraction since 1997. (REIC Q1 2025 report; Nation Thailand, 2025)

4. Chiang Mai's active listing market in 2026 is roughly **40–45% condos/apartments, 35–40% detached houses, 15–20% townhouses** by listing count (DDProperty/Hipflat/FazWaz listing analysis, Bamboo Routes, 2026).

### Price Levels

5. **Condo prices per sqm in central Chiang Mai (2025–2026):**
   - Nimman/Suthep: ฿80,000–140,000/sqm
   - Old City: ฿75,000–120,000/sqm
   - Chang Khlan (Riverside): ฿70,000–160,000/sqm (widest range)
   - Santitham: ฿50,000–85,000/sqm
   - Chang Phueak: ฿55,000–95,000/sqm
   - Suburban (Hang Dong, San Sai): ฿20,000–45,000/sqm
   - Market median across all types: ~฿60,000/sqm; average ~฿62,000/sqm
   (Bamboo Routes, citing Bank of Thailand RPPI, Dot Property, FazWaz, Jan 2026)

6. **Average house price: ~฿4.5 million** (~$125,000). **80% of purchases fall between ฿2–9 million** (Bamboo Routes, Apr 2026).

7. **Land prices:** Hang Dong ฿4–8 million/rai (higher for road-frontage/mountain-view plots). San Sai ~฿5 million/rai median. Suburban fringe land as low as ฿500,000/rai. (Portal listing analysis, 2025)

8. **New high-end condos** by leading Thai developers in central Chiang Mai start at **฿3+ million per unit** for 1-bed units. (DDProperty/Siam Real Estate listings, 2024–2025)

9. **Price appreciation (12-month, 2025–2026):** Prime condos (Nimman, Chang Phueak) +6–8%; suburban houses/townhouses +3–5%; northern region overall +1.84% — the **smallest regional gain nationally** in Q2 2025. (DDProperty market index Q2 2025; Bamboo Routes, 2026)

10. **10-year nominal appreciation (2016–2026):** ~45% in Chiang Mai, ~25% inflation-adjusted. (Bamboo Routes, Jan 2026)

### Rental Market

11. **Monthly rents (furnished, 2026):**
   - Studio (23–30 sqm): ฿7,000–12,000 avg ฿9,500/mo
   - 1-bedroom (28–40 sqm): ฿10,000–18,000 avg ฿13,500/mo
   - 2-bedroom (50–80 sqm): ฿16,000–35,000 avg ฿22,000/mo
   - Houses in Hang Dong for families: ฿20,000–45,000/mo
   Nimman and Chang Khlan command the highest rents; Mae Hia and San Sai the lowest.
   (Bamboo Routes rents update, Jan 2026 / AirDNA short-term data, 2026)

12. **Rental yields:** Long-term 4–7% overall; Nimman/Old City 6–8% (strong nomad/expat demand). Short-term Airbnb/Agoda 8–12% in prime locations, but occupancy is seasonal: 55–65% annually, peaking November–February, troughing March–May (smoke/heat). (Bamboo Routes, 2026)

13. **75–80% of tenants in Chiang Mai prefer furnished units.** Furnished premium: ฿2,000–4,000/month extra. Top tenant profiles: Thai students 40–50%, expat nomads 25–35%, retirees 15–20%. (Bamboo Routes, 2026)

14. **Rents grew 4–6% year-on-year** (Jan 2025 to Jan 2026). Rental demand rose while fewer people could access mortgages, reflecting a behavioral shift from purchase to rental among those locked out of credit. (Bamboo Routes / Nation Thailand, 2025–2026)

### Buyer Segments

15. **Chinese buyers dominate foreign purchases** in Chiang Mai. Over the past decade, mainland Chinese bought **over 1,000 housing units** worth **฿3–5 billion** concentrated in Hang Dong, San Kamphaeng, and San Sai districts; units typically priced ฿3 million+. In the Chiang Mai Real Estate Association's 2024 data, Chinese buyers "outstrip not just other foreigners but even Thai nationals." *(Caveat: the Chiang Mai RE Association report, as cited via BenaNews, does not specify the denominator — it almost certainly refers to the foreign-buyer or investor-active cohort in specific districts, not all residential purchases city-wide. Thai nationals remain the overwhelmingly dominant buyer group in the overall Chiang Mai residential market. Treat this claim as directionally accurate for the ฿3M+ investor segment in those three districts, not as a citywide demographic reversal.)* (Chiang Mai Real Estate Association 2024, cited BenaNews Nov 2024 + Nation Thailand 2024)

16. **Nationally, China led foreign condo purchases** in Jan–Sep 2024 (Q1–Q3 2024). Chiang Mai recorded **698 condo transfers to foreign buyers** in that period, placing it 4th nationally after Bangkok, Chonburi, and Phuket. *(Primary source: REIC transfer records, Q1–Q3 2024; reported via Nation Thailand Sep 2025 and KhaoSod English Mar 2026.)* However, foreign condo transfers in Chiang Mai **fell 28%** in 2025, attributed to "economic uncertainty in China and safety concerns." Myanmar buyers are rising as a second tier, driven by middle-class families seeking security and education. US buyer activity rose significantly following a new US Consulate opening in Chiang Mai (staff scaling 200 → 1,000+). (REIC Q1–Q3 2024; Nation Thailand Sep 2025; BenaNews Nov 2024)

17. **Chinese buyer motivations** in purchasing Thai property nationally (2566–2568 survey data): vacation/leisure 31.8%, investment 27.4%, education 21.1%, relocation 14.3%, retirement 5.4%. Thailand ranks 5th globally for Chinese overseas property purchases (after US, UK, Canada, Australia). (Bangkok Biz News, citing survey data, 2024)

18. **Chinese investor legal mechanisms** used to circumvent the 49% foreign condo quota and land ownership ban: Thai nominee structures, Chinese-run Thai agencies, marriages to Thai nationals, bulk purchasing then reselling to Chinese buyers. Industry president: "Without any intervention, all properties in Chiang Mai will eventually be bought up by foreign investors." (Nation Thailand; BenaNews, 2024)

19. **Digital nomads and retirees** create consistent long-term rental demand, particularly in Nimman, Old City, and Riverside (Wat Ket). Chiang Mai ranks as a top global digital nomad base due to cost-of-living, co-working density, and reliable fiber internet. No official count of nomad residents exists; community estimates suggest 5,000–20,000 active at any time (unverified). (Bamboo Routes; multiple EN-language nomad guides, 2024–2026)

20. **Thai families and local buyers** — government workers, doctors, CMU staff — are the stable demand base for the ฿2–5 million detached house segment. Bangkok second-home investors are active in the ฿3–6 million condo segment. Pantip community data shows buyers searching in ฿3–4 million detached house budget; concerns include pricing inconsistency between agents and high foreclosure inventory. (Pantip threads 38646478, 36465943, 2023–2024; DDProperty listing analysis)

### Data Sources for AVM

21. **Treasury Department appraisal prices (กรมธนารักษ์):**
   - **URL:** assessprice.treasury.go.th (lookup) and catalog.treasury.go.th/dataset/land-valuation (bulk download)
   - **Coverage:** All 77 provinces, including Chiang Mai; land, condos, and structures
   - **Format:** CSV per province; ArcGIS map layer for Chiang Mai exists
   - **Update frequency:** Annual (last updated Nov 2025 for 2025 cycle)
   - **API:** No public API; web portal + direct CSV download only
   - **License:** "Open Data Common" — the label shown on catalog.treasury.go.th; governed by Thailand's DGA Open Government License (data.go.th); no access restrictions
   - **Gap:** Appraisal values are typically **20–40% below market** depending on location and appraisal cycle vintage (practitioner consensus: Phuket News, Siam Legal, Conrad Properties; quadrennial review cycle means values lag current market). Useful as floor/baseline, not for market-rate AVM. The upper tail (rural or long-lapsed parcels) can reach higher discounts, but 50% is not documented in published sources — use 20–40% as the defensible range.

22. **REIC (Real Estate Information Center):**
   - **Coverage:** National residential transfers (units + value), housing construction permits, unsold inventory, developer sentiment index, vacant land price index, housing purchase confidence index
   - **Geographic granularity:** Provincial-level at minimum; some district-level in Bangkok reports
   - **Data inputs:** Land Department transfer records, BMA, NSO, Bank of Thailand mortgage data
   - **Access:** Annual and quarterly reports free on reic.or.th; raw microdata not publicly available; API not advertised
   - **Best use for AVM:** Transfer volume and price indices for market trend direction; comparable transaction counts by province

23. **Bank of Thailand RPPI:**
   - **Coverage:** Single houses + townhouses (national); condominiums (Bangkok/vicinity only — not Chiang Mai condos)
   - **Update:** Monthly, one-month lag
   - **Methodology:** 12-month rolling hedonic regression with quality adjustment; 3-month smoothing
   - **Access:** app.bot.or.th/BTWS_STAT/statistics (reportID=920), free, downloadable
   - **Gap for CNX AVM:** Condo sub-index does not cover Chiang Mai; only useful for house/townhouse price trend direction

24. **AREA (Agency for Real Estate Affairs — area.co.th):**
   - Founded 1982; Thailand's longest-running private property research firm
   - Tracks Bangkok market primarily (31 housing estates including condos); limited documented Chiang Mai coverage
   - GIS database of millions of properties across Thailand and ASEAN
   - Data access: primarily subscription/consultancy basis; not free download
   - **Best use:** Bangkok hedonic comparables; methodology benchmarking; limited CNX utility

25. **Portal indices (DDProperty, Baania, FazWaz, Hipflat):**
   - **DDProperty Property Market Index:** Quarterly asking-price index; covers Chiang Mai as a named province in regional breakdowns; published as free PDF/article reports. Reflects listing prices, not transaction prices (upward bias). No public bulk data download or API documented.
   - **Baania (baaniathailand.com/data/):** Provides APIs for real-time asking prices + listing counts + consumer pageviews; free Open Data downloads via gobestimate.com/data; office in Chiang Mai. Most accessible programmatic data source for AVM development. Coverage: Thailand only.
   - **FazWaz/Hipflat:** Regional portals with listing databases; Chiang Mai well covered. No documented open API; data typically accessed via scraping or commercial partnership.
   - **Best use for AVM:** Baania API for real-time comparables; DDProperty quarterly reports for trend calibration

## 4. Heuristics

**MKT-01: Price display must lead with total price in THB, with sqm price as secondary.** 80%+ of Thai buyers think in total budget (฿2–9M range covers most of market). Price-per-sqm is a comparison tool, not a primary filter. Schema: `asking_price_thb` (required) + `size_sqm` (required for condo) → derived `price_per_sqm_thb` display field.

**MKT-02: Furnishing status is a first-class filter field, not a text note.** 75–80% of renters require furnished units; an unfurnished listing at the same price point will receive near-zero rental inquiries from expats, nomads, and retirees. Schema: `furnishing_status: enum['unfurnished','partial','full']` required for all rental listings.

**MKT-03: Rental listings must display monthly rate as primary CTA price; never per-night or per-day.** Nomad and expat demand — the dominant foreign rental segment — searches monthly. Per-night framing triggers "hotel-mode" mental model and devalues long-stay offerings.

**MKT-04: Every listing must carry a `district` (อำเภอ) field in addition to `province`.** CNX buyer decisions are district-level (Hang Dong vs San Sai vs Nimman area = wildly different price/lifestyle). Province-only granularity is useless for search and AVM. Minimum: province + district + sub-district (ตำบล) for land listings.

**MKT-05: Surface "time on market" and absorption context to all buyers.** With 57-month citywide absorption, buyers need anchoring context. A listing sitting 90+ days at asking is either overpriced or a distressed asset — both are buyer signals. Schema: `listed_date` required; auto-compute days on market; flag >90-day listings.

**MKT-06: Do not use Treasury Department appraisal price as estimated market value in any public-facing display.** Appraisals run 20–40% below transaction prices (practitioner-documented range; quadrennial review cycle causes structural lag); displaying them as "official value" misleads buyers and exposes the platform to disputes. Use only internally as AVM floor constraint.

**MKT-07: AVM v1 should be built on Baania API + Treasury CSV + DDProperty asking-price trend, not on BOT RPPI alone.** BOT RPPI excludes Chiang Mai condos. Baania offers programmatic access to asking prices. Treasury CSV gives parcel-level appraisal data. This combination covers all three inputs a hedonic model needs: comparables, location baseline, trend direction.

**MKT-08: Listing cards for the Chinese buyer segment must include property type eligibility clearly.** Foreign nationals can legally own condo units (up to 49% of building floor area); they cannot own land or houses in their own name. Any listing that is land or a house must display a clear "foreign ownership: not eligible" label — failure to do so creates legal risk and erodes trust.

**MKT-09: For the nomad/expat rental segment, lead with neighborhood lifestyle signal, not property specs.** Nomads choose Nimman for cafes + coworking, Old City for walkability, Riverside for quiet. Schema: `neighborhood_slug` mapped to curated lifestyle tags shown on listing card. Spec-first cards (sqm, floor, year built) perform poorly with this segment.

**MKT-10: Smoke season (Feb–Apr) is a material listing risk; disclose it in property descriptions and surface in seasonal search copy.** Chiang Mai's air quality regularly hits hazardous AQI during burning season. Buyers/renters who discover this post-commitment feel deceived. Editorial honesty is a competitive differentiator vs. portals that suppress this.

**MKT-11: Distressed/foreclosure inventory is large enough to justify a dedicated filter.** Pantip community data shows explicit buyer demand for "บ้านยึด/บังคับคดี" (foreclosure) assets at discount. Schema: `listing_type: enum['standard','developer_new','foreclosure','short_sale']`. Validate with broker group whether tagging these creates legal or relationship complications.

**MKT-12: Price tier ฿3–5M is the sweet spot where Thai domestic buyers, Bangkok second-home investors, and entry-level foreign buyers (Chinese, Myanmar) all compete.** This is the critical tier for the exclusivity/opt-in mechanics of the platform — broker incentive to keep listings private is highest here because competition is most acute. Reflect in the default sort order and editorial content strategy.

**MKT-13: Myanmar buyers are a rising second-tier segment in Chiang Mai, driven by relocation for safety, education, and healthcare — not tourism.** Mandalay/Yangon→CNX is a natural corridor (proximity + direct flights + cultural similarity). REIC data confirms Myanmar reached 2nd place nationally among foreign condo buyers in first 9 months of 2024, with buyers in Chiang Mai targeting detached houses at ฿20–30M for family relocation (Nation Thailand; Bangkok Post / REIC, 2024–2025). Add Burmese (မြန်မာဘာသာ) as a future-language consideration in the localization backlog; current Thai+English launch is correct, but Myanmar is the next highest-ROI language for Northern Thailand specifically. (Note: the national avg ฿6.5M/unit figure cited in some reports is a Bangkok-skewed condo-transfer statistic; the CNX-specific Myanmar segment targets higher-value houses, per REIC/Colliers data.)

## 5. Anti-Patterns

1. **"Bangkok data as proxy for Chiang Mai."** BOT's condo RPPI is Bangkok/vicinity only. National transfer statistics mask CNX's sharper 2025 contraction. Using national or Bangkok figures as CNX benchmarks will systematically mislead AVM and editorial claims.

2. **"Quoting appraisal price as market price."** Treasury appraisals lag market by 1–3 cycles and are structurally below transaction prices. Several Thai RE portals display "ราคาประเมิน" as if it were market value — it is not, and it actively misleads first-time buyers.

3. **"Chasing the Chinese buyer wave."** Chinese buyer demand in Chiang Mai is real but already peaking and legally contested. Building core acquisition flows around this segment (e.g., Mandarin-first UI, bulk listing deals) exposes the platform to regulatory risk and demand volatility. The segment is better served as a filter facet, not a product pillar.

4. **"Portal asking prices as transaction prices."** DDProperty, FazWaz, and Hipflat list asking prices. Actual transaction prices run 3–7% below asking on average; in slow segments up to 15% below. An AVM that trains on listing prices without a discount haircut will systematically overvalue.

5. **"Assuming rental yield is stable year-round."** Short-term rental occupancy swings from 55–65% annually with November–February peaks. Quoting annual average yield without seasonal disclosure misleads investors comparing to fixed-income alternatives.

6. **"Treating Chiang Mai as a single market."** Nimman (฿80K–140K/sqm condo) and Doi Saket (฿20K–35K/sqm) are not in the same market. Neighborhood-blind search and valuation is the dominant failure mode on current portals.

7. **"Assuming English-only suffices for Thai buyers."** Pantip threads confirm Thai buyers research and discuss property purchases in Thai, expect Thai-language property descriptions, and are skeptical of broker pricing. The platform's Thai-language quality is a direct trust signal to the most important buyer segment.

## 7. Ask the Market

These questions require validation with actual brokers and users in the LINE groups:

1. **What price tier do brokers actually refuse to share publicly?** Is ฿3–5M the true exclusivity sweet spot, or is it ฿5–15M luxury where brokers most want private-first distribution?

2. **How do brokers today handle the Chinese buyer segment?** Do they work with Chinese agents directly? Use LINE groups? Is there a separate Chinese-language broker network in CNX that we'd need to integrate or compete with?

3. **What do Thai families actually search for that portals fail to provide?** Pantip data shows frustration with pricing inconsistency. Is the gap a search facet problem (e.g., no school catchment filter, no flood zone filter) or a trust/verification problem?

4. **How active is the Chiang Mai foreclosure market as a buyer opportunity?** Pantip signals strong interest but no volume data exists. Are brokers actively listing these, or are they sold through separate auction channels (Legal Execution Department / bank NPA portals)?

5. **What is the actual monthly rent corridor for digital nomads?** The ฿10,000–18,000 range for 1-bedroom is widely cited but may reflect 2022–2023 data. Has the nomad influx post-COVID driven Nimman rents above ฿20,000 for quality units?

6. **Is there a viable Thai-language listing standard brokers already use in LINE groups?** If brokers in our target groups are already posting in a consistent format (e.g., price / size / bedrooms / location / contact), our extraction schema may already be partially validated — or we may need to accommodate highly variable formats.

7. **How do Myanmar buyers actually find and transact on Chiang Mai properties?** Via Chinese broker networks? Thai agents? Direct? This affects whether the Myanmar segment is accessible through our platform or operates in a parallel informal market.
